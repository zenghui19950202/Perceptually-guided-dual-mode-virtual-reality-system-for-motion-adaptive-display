Research Overview

Understanding Visual Search

Visual search is a fundamental cognitive process that allows humans to locate specific targets within a cluttered environment. Traditional interfaces rely on manual interactions, while gaze-contingent interfaces leverage eye tracking technology to enhance interaction efficiency.

This research investigates whether gaze-contingent visual search can reduce cognitive load and improve task performance compared to traditional methods.

Research Question

"Does gaze-contingent interaction improve visual search performance in terms of speed, accuracy, and perceived cognitive load compared to traditional mouse-based interaction?"

24 Participants

Mixed gender, ages 18-35

Per participant

Figure 1: Experimental setup with eye tracking equipment

Research Method

Participants

24 participants (12 male, 12 female) aged between 18 and 35 years with normal or corrected-to-normal vision.

Apparatus

Tobii Pro Spectrum eye tracker (120 Hz sampling rate), 24-inch LCD monitor (1920×1080 resolution), and standard keyboard and mouse.

Tasks

Participants performed visual search tasks using both traditional mouse-based interaction and gaze-contingent interaction with different levels of visual complexity.

Experimental Design

Pre-Experiment

Participants completed a training session to familiarize themselves with the task and equipment. Baseline measurements were taken.

Visual Search Tasks

Participants performed 320 trials of visual search tasks in both traditional and gaze-contingent conditions, counterbalanced to control for order effects.

Questionnaires

After each condition, participants completed the NASA-TLX questionnaire to assess perceived cognitive load and a SUS questionnaire for system usability.

Data Analysis

Reaction times, error rates, and questionnaire responses were analyzed using repeated measures ANOVA and paired t-tests.
